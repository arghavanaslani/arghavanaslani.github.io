## Summary

Compte walks through the classic Wang (2001) attractor model of working memory: a network of excitatory and inhibitory neurons where recurrent excitation creates persistent activity "bumps" that encode stimulus locations. The talk emphasizes both the elegance of the model and its empirical problems (too much persistent activity, too noise-sensitive).

The second half covers more recent alternatives — including the synaptic weight / activity-silent models — and frames open questions around what "maintenance" really means biologically.

## Key quotes

> "The question is not whether persistent activity exists — it clearly does in some conditions. The question is whether it is doing the work we thought it was doing."

> "A model that fits the data is not the same as a model that explains the brain. But it's a start."

## What I took away

The framing of attractor models as a *class* of hypotheses rather than a single model was clarifying. The distinction between content-addressable memory (what is stored) and timing mechanisms (when it is retrieved) feels important for my own work.
